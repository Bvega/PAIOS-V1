For a song like *“You Go, Then I’ll Go”* — built on unity, symbolism, and a simple but powerful phrase — the style matters if you want **maximum reach** across audiences.  

Here are the top contenders with their strengths:

---

## 1. **Pop Anthem (Mainstream / Billboard Style)**
- **Why:** Pop is the widest-reaching genre globally, especially when fused with uplifting themes. Think of songs like *“Firework”* (Katy Perry) or *“Stronger”* (Kelly Clarkson).  
- **Sound:** Big choruses, layered vocals, modern production with electronic beats and real instruments.  
- **Reach:** Radio-friendly, TikTok-usable, and works on major streaming playlists.  

---

## 2. **Pop-Rock Americana (Springsteen / Coldplay style)**
- **Why:** Taps into *American unity* and tradition. Guitars + driving drums create stadium energy. Perfect for live performances and national events (sports, 4th of July, charity concerts).  
- **Sound:** Anthemic, sing-along hooks with folk-rock heart.  
- **Reach:** Strong in the U.S. and Europe; connects emotionally with cross-generational listeners.  

---

## 3. **Gospel-Pop Crossover (Alicia Keys / John Legend feel)**
- **Why:** Choir-driven choruses scream “unity.” Gospel fusion adds authenticity and emotional power.  
- **Sound:** Piano foundation, R&B vocals, gospel choir layering.  
- **Reach:** Cuts across cultural and racial lines; powerful in moments of crisis/unity.  

---

## 4. **Country-Pop (Modern Nashville Style)**
- **Why:** Country audiences resonate with themes of road, patience, community, America. With a pop crossover twist (like Kacey Musgraves or Keith Urban), it can go wide.  
- **Sound:** Acoustic guitars, harmonies, mid-tempo build.  
- **Reach:** Strong U.S. impact, but slightly less global than pop.  

---

## 5. **EDM-Pop Anthem (Avicii / Kygo style)**
- **Why:** High-energy, festival-ready, perfect for streaming and viral reach. Unity themes + drop = massive crowd energy.  
- **Sound:** Synth build-ups, vocal chops on “You go, then I’ll go,” big festival drops.  
- **Reach:** Global, youth-heavy, viral potential.

Good catch — this happens because **Suno sometimes reads bracketed text literally as lyrics** instead of interpreting them as metadata or stage directions.  
To fix that, you have three reliable formatting options depending on which version of Suno you’re using:

---

### ✅ **Option 1 — Use Double Brackets for Meta Tags**
Wrap all *style* or *technical* info in **double brackets `[[ ... ]]`** instead of single ones.  
Suno will treat them as stage or context notes instead of singable text.

**Example fix:**
```text
[[Intro – 80 BPM A minor – ambient industrial FX, reversed samples]]
You go… then I’ll go…
```

---

### ✅ **Option 2 — Move Tags to the “Style” Field**
Put the global style tags and BPM/key info in Suno’s **Style box** (not in the lyric body).  
Then leave only clean lyric and vocal cues in the main prompt.

**Example:**
- **Lyrics field:**
  ```
  (Intro)
  You go… then I’ll go…
  ```

- **Style field:**
  ```
  Urban anthem / industrial funk / gospel drill fusion. 
  80–144 BPM transitions. Male rap + female contralto duet.

  ```

### ✅ **Option 3 — Use Parentheses or Curly Braces for Stage Notes**

**Example fix:**
```text
(Intro – ambient subway FX, 80 BPM, A minor)
```

Suno ignores most parentheses during lyric parsing.
