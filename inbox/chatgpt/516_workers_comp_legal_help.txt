USER:
I had an injury in my left shoulder a year ago , my doctor said I has no being healed properly and that I need to go to surgery, and I just had and independent evaluation required by the works comp insurance company, the report says that never the less I will have some condition because of the trauma I am entitled to go back to full work, what should I do?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ASSISTANT:
Your situation is common in workers’ compensation cases, where the insurance company’s independent medical evaluation (IME) may downplay the severity of your condition. Here’s what you can do next:  

### **1. Review the IME Report in Detail**  
- Obtain a copy of the IME report.  
- Compare it with your treating doctor’s recommendations, especially regarding surgery and work restrictions.  
- Look for inconsistencies or inaccuracies. If the IME report contradicts your doctor’s findings, this can be challenged.  

### **2. Follow Your Treating Doctor’s Recommendations**  
- Your treating physician, who has been monitoring your condition, holds more weight in many cases than the IME doctor, who only saw you once.  
- If your doctor still believes you need surgery and cannot return to full work duties, follow their recommendations.  

### **3. Do NOT Return to Work If You Are Physically Unable**  
- If you return to full duty while injured, it could worsen your condition and weaken your case.  
- Discuss with your doctor whether you need work restrictions or a modified duty recommendation.  
- If your employer pressures you to return against medical advice, document everything in writing.  

### **4. Challenge the IME Report**  
- You or your lawyer can formally dispute the IME report with the workers’ compensation board.  
- You may request a second opinion or an independent medical review.  
- Your doctor can submit a strong rebuttal explaining why the IME findings are incorrect.  

### **5. Consider Legal Representation**  
- If the insurance company denies surgery or forces you back to work, consult a **workers’ compensation lawyer** immediately.  
- A lawyer can request a **workers’ compensation hearing** to argue your case and push for medical treatment approval.  

### **6. Document Everything**  
- Keep records of all medical visits, recommendations, and communication with your employer or the insurance company.  
- Any worsening symptoms should be documented and reported to your doctor.  

### **7. Request a Functional Capacity Evaluation (FCE)**  
- If needed, your doctor can order an **FCE**, which objectively assesses whether you can return to work.  

### **Bottom Line**  
If your treating doctor recommends surgery and believes you are not fit for full work duties, **do not accept the IME findings without challenging them**. You have the right to appeal and seek proper medical care. If the insurance company resists, **a workers' comp attorney can fight for your rights and benefits.** Let me know if you need help drafting a dispute letter or preparing for a hearing.

ASSISTANT:
Here’s a **detailed list of insights and questions** to discuss with your treating doctor during your appointment:

### **1. Medical Condition & Diagnosis**  
- What is the current status of my shoulder injury?  
- Has my condition worsened, improved, or remained the same over the past year?  
- What specific damage or complications do you see in my shoulder?  
- Are there any long-term effects expected from this injury?  

### **2. Treatment Plan & Surgery**  
- Do you still recommend surgery as the best option for my recovery?  
- What are the risks and expected outcomes of the surgery?  
- What happens if I delay or avoid surgery?  
- How long is the estimated recovery period post-surgery?  
- What type of physical therapy or rehabilitation will I need after surgery?  

### **3. Work Restrictions & Ability to Work**  
- Am I physically able to return to full-duty work?  
- What specific work restrictions should I follow (e.g., no lifting, limited movement, light duty)?  
- Would returning to work too soon worsen my condition or delay my recovery?  
- Can you provide a detailed **work restrictions letter** if needed?  

### **4. Independent Medical Evaluation (IME) Discrepancies**  
- The IME report says I can return to full work, but you recommended surgery—can you explain why your findings differ?  
- Can you **write a rebuttal** to dispute the IME report?  
- Are there any tests (MRI, X-rays, functional capacity evaluation) you can order to support your medical opinion?  
- Do you recommend getting a second opinion from another specialist?  

### **5. Workers’ Compensation & Insurance Approval**  
- Will you help me request **pre-authorization for surgery** from the workers' comp insurer?  
- What should I do if workers’ comp denies or delays my surgery?  
- Do you have experience dealing with workers' comp disputes, and can you provide supporting medical reports for my case?  

### **6. Permanent Impairment & Disability Considerations**  
- Will my shoulder fully recover, or am I likely to have a **permanent impairment**?  
- If I have a permanent limitation, should I request a **permanent partial disability (PPD) rating**?  
- Can you provide documentation for any long-term limitations affecting my job?  

### **7. Next Steps & Documentation**  
- Can you provide a **detailed medical report** outlining my diagnosis, treatment plan, and need for surgery?  
- Can you prepare an **updated work restrictions letter** for my employer and insurance company?  
- What follow-up tests or treatments should I schedule next?  

### **Bonus Tip:** **Request Copies of All Medical Records**  
- Ask for a copy of your **latest medical report, imaging results, and work restrictions letter** for your own records.  
- This documentation will be critical if you need to challenge the IME report or hire a workers' comp attorney.  

Let me know if you need help drafting any formal requests or letters after your appointment.
