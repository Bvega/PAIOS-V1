If a person's W-2 income for the year is only $560 and they also receive workers' compensation, the tax process depends on how their income and workers' compensation benefits interact under IRS rules. Here's a breakdown:

### Workers' Compensation and Taxes
1. **Workers' Compensation Benefits**:
   - Workers' compensation benefits are **not taxable** at the federal level (and in most states) if they are paid under a workers' compensation act or statute for job-related injuries or illnesses.
   - These benefits should not appear on the W-2 or be included in taxable income.

2. **W-2 Income ($560)**:
   - If the person's total W-2 income is only $560 for the year, they are far below the **standard deduction** threshold for filing taxes. For example, the 2023 standard deduction is:
     - $13,850 for single filers.
     - $27,700 for married filing jointly.
   - Since the person's income does not exceed the standard deduction, they likely won’t owe taxes or even need to file a tax return (unless they are required to file for other reasons, such as self-employment income or certain tax credits).

### Filing a Tax Return
Even though filing may not be required, the individual may still want to file a tax return if:
- **They had taxes withheld**: If any federal income taxes were withheld from the $560 (check Box 2 on the W-2), they may file a tax return to get a refund of the withheld taxes.
- **They are eligible for credits**: They could qualify for refundable tax credits like the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), even with low income. For 2023, a single filer without children earning up to $17,640 could qualify for the EITC.

### Key Considerations
1. **Social Security and Medicare (FICA)**:
   - The W-2 income of $560 likely had Social Security and Medicare taxes withheld (Box 4 and Box 6). Workers' compensation benefits do not count as income for Social Security purposes, so they don't contribute to Social Security or Medicare.

2. **Combined Benefits**:
   - If the person received other benefits like Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) along with workers' compensation, part of the SSDI might be taxable. However, workers' compensation itself would still not be taxed.

3. **State Taxes**:
   - Some states have specific rules, so the person should verify state-level tax obligations if applicable.

### Conclusion
- With only $560 in W-2 income and non-taxable workers' compensation benefits, the person likely won't owe taxes and may not need to file a tax return. However, they should file if they want to claim a refund of withheld taxes or qualify for credits like the EITC.
- They should review their W-2 and workers' compensation documents or consult with a tax professional to confirm their specific situation.
