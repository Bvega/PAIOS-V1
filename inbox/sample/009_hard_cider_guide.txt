ASSISTANT:
Here’s a **professional yet simple home-scale guide** to turn your **1 gallon of pasteurized apple cider juice** into a clean, balanced **hard cider** (≈ 6 – 7 % ABV).  
This method assumes you have basic equipment (fermenter, airlock, siphon, hydrometer, sanitizer).

---

## 🍎 Stage 1 – Preparation (Day 0)

### 1. Equipment & sanitation  
- **Primary fermenter:** 1 gal glass jug or food-grade plastic bucket with airlock.  
- **Sanitize** everything that touches the juice (Starsan or potassium metabisulfite solution).  
  *Cleanliness = 80 % of cider quality.*

### 2. Base juice  
- 1 gal **pasteurized** apple cider (no preservatives like sorbate or benzoate).  
- Bring to room temp (65–70 °F / 18–21 °C).  
- Take a **specific gravity (SG)** reading — usually ≈ 1.045 – 1.055.  
  - Optional: add sugar or honey to reach SG 1.060 – 1.065 for ~6.5 % ABV.

---

## ⚗️ Stage 2 – Juice Conditioning (Day 0)

| Additive | Amount per 1 gal | Purpose | When to add |
|-----------|------------------|----------|--------------|
| **Campden tablet (potassium metabisulfite)** | 1 tablet, crushed | Kills wild yeast/bacteria | Immediately |
| **Pectic enzyme** | ½ tsp | Breaks pectin haze for clear cider | After Campden, wait 12 h |
| **Acid blend** | ¼ tsp (optional) | Balances flat juice | Same time as pectic enzyme |
| **Ascorbic acid (Vit C)** | pinch (⅛ tsp) | Antioxidant, color retention | Same time |
| **Wine tannin** | ⅛ tsp (optional) | Adds body & bite | Same time |

➡ Stir gently, cover loosely, and **let sit 12 hours** before pitching yeast so sulfite can dissipate.

---

## 🍾 Stage 3 – Primary Fermentation (Days 1–14)

1. **Yeast choice:**  
   - *Safale S-04* (clean English cider)  
   - *Lalvin D47* (fragrant, semi-dry)  
   - *Nottingham Ale yeast* (neutral & fast)  

2. **Pitch:** Sprinkle ½ packet directly into the juice (or rehydrate).  
3. **Seal with airlock** and keep at **65–70 °F (18–21 °C)**.  
4. Fermentation starts in 24–36 h; bubbling slows after 7–10 days.  
5. When bubbles stop, check SG ≈ 1.000 or below → fermentation complete.

---

## 🍶 Stage 4 – Racking & Clearing (Days 14–28)

1. **Rack** (siphon) into a clean, sanitized jug, leaving sediment behind.  
2. Optional: Add ¼ Campden + ¼ tsp potassium sorbate if you plan to back-sweeten.  
3. Store cool (55 °F / 13 °C) 2–4 weeks to clarify.  
4. When clear, taste:  
   - Too tart → add ½ tsp sugar per glass before serving.  
   - Too flat → carbonate (next stage).

---

## 💨 Stage 5 – Bottling (Day 28+)

### Still cider  
- Simply bottle in sanitized wine bottles, cork/cap, and age 2–3 weeks before drinking.

### Sparkling cider  
1. Dissolve **1 oz (≈ 28 g)** priming sugar in ¼ cup hot water.  
2. Add to bottling bucket, rack cider onto it, stir gently.  
3. Bottle in beer bottles, cap, and keep at **room temp (~70 °F)** for 7–10 days.  
4. Chill, test carbonation, then refrigerate.

---

## 🧊 Stage 6 – Conditioning & Aging

- **Early taste (3–4 weeks)**: light, fresh, still yeasty.  
- **Best after 8–12 weeks**: smooth, integrated, bright apple aroma.  
- Store cool and dark; refrigerate after carbonation.

---

## 🔬 Quick Reference Table

| Parameter | Target |
|------------|---------|
| Original Gravity | 1.060 ± 0.005 |
| Final Gravity | 0.998 – 1.005 |
| ABV | ~6–7 % |
| Fermentation Temp | 65–70 °F / 18–21 °C |
| Primary Duration | 10–14 days |
| Clearing/Aging | 2–4 weeks |

ASSISTANT:
That’s a **classic vigorous fermentation overflow**, often called a **blow-off** — and it’s actually a *good sign* that your yeast is active. Here’s exactly what to do, step-by-step, to save your batch safely:  

---

### ⚠️ 1. Don’t panic — do not throw it out
This means fermentation is strong and healthy. You just need to relieve the pressure and clean up correctly.

---

### 🧽 2. Clean and sanitize immediately
- Wipe away any spilled cider or foam around the mouth and outside of the jug.  
- Remove the **airlock or siphon tube** and clean/sanitize it thoroughly.  
- If krausen (foam) got inside, rinse with warm water, sanitize again, and reattach.

---

### 🧯 3. Switch to a blow-off setup (temporary)
If it overflowed once, it’ll likely do it again.  
Do this:

1. Take a **3/8" or ½" flexible hose** that fits snugly where your airlock was.  
2. Insert the hose into the bung or lid hole.  
3. Place the other end of the hose in a jar of **sanitizer or water** — this lets CO₂ escape while blocking air backflow.  
4. Keep the jar slightly below the fermenter to let foam drain out safely.

> This is called a **blow-off tube** — standard practice for the first 2–3 days of cider or beer fermentation.

---

### 🌡️ 4. Check temperature
Fermentation can get **too hot** and become over-vigorous.  
- Ideal range: **65–70 °F (18–21 °C)**.  
- If higher, move to a cooler place (basement, tile floor, or near AC vent).  
- Cooler temps calm the foam and produce cleaner flavor.

---

### 🍏 5. Replace airlock after 3–4 days
Once bubbling slows and krausen falls, remove the blow-off tube, clean and sanitize your airlock, and reattach it.

---

### 💧 6. Top up if you lost liquid
If you lost more than ~½ cup of cider, top up with **a bit of sanitized apple juice** to bring the volume back to ~1 gallon.  
Don’t add more sugar now — that would restart fermentation too strongly.

---

### 🧼 7. Sanitize everything you touched
Anything that touched cider or foam must be sanitized again — contamination risk is highest right now.

---

ASSISTANT:
Excellent observation — that’s very important to catch early.  
If the **cap or airlock seal was broken for days**, oxygen and possibly microbes could have entered.  
But don’t worry yet — we can **assess and rescue** the batch safely.  

Here’s exactly what to do:  

---

## 🧪 1. **Inspect Before Touching Anything**
- **Look:** Is there **mold**, fuzzy growth, or white film (“pellicle”) on top?  
  - ✅ If *no mold* and cider looks/smells fine → probably safe.  
  - ❌ If *fuzzy white/green patches* or sour/rotten smell → discard.  

- **Smell:**  
  - *Normal:* fruity, yeasty, or slightly sulfuric.  
  - *Bad:* vinegar, rotten eggs, or nail polish remover = infection.

---

## ⚗️ 2. **Take a Gravity Reading**
Use a **hydrometer** if possible:  
- If **SG ≤ 1.000**, fermentation likely finished — yeast already consumed sugar, so oxidation risk is limited.  
- If **SG > 1.010**, it might have stalled due to oxygen exposure or temperature drop.

---

## 🔄 3. **If Gravity Is Stable and No Infection**
Do this to stabilize and protect the cider:  
1. **Rack** (siphon) cider carefully into a **sanitized jug**, leaving sediment behind.  
2. Add:  
   - **1 crushed Campden tablet (potassium metabisulfite)** → removes oxygen and kills contaminants.  
   - Optional: **¼ tsp potassium sorbate** if you plan to back-sweeten.  
3. Attach a **fresh sanitized airlock** and seal tightly.  
4. Let it sit **1–2 weeks** in a cool dark place to finish clarifying.

---

## 🧫 4. **If You Suspect Contamination**
If there’s *film, cloudiness that returns after racking,* or off-odors:
1. Rack immediately into clean sanitized jug.  
2. Add **1 Campden tablet per gallon**.  
3. Stir gently, seal, and store cool (50–60 °F / 10–15 °C).  
4. Taste after 5 days — if flavor improves, keep it; if sour/bitter, dump it safely.  

---

## 🌡️ 5. **Prevent for Future Batches**
- Always use a **tight cap/airlock seal**; replace any cracked rubber bungs.  
- Consider **blow-off tubing** for first 5 days, then switch to airlock.  
- Keep fermenter in a place with **stable temperature and minimal movement.**

ASSISTANT:
Excellent — this is one of the most beautiful stages of cider making.  
Clarification (also called **clearing** or **polishing**) is the process where your cider transitions from cloudy and yeasty to bright, golden, and clean-tasting.  

Here’s the professional breakdown 👇  

---

## 🍏 1. **What Happens Naturally**

After fermentation, cider contains:
- **Suspended yeast cells**
- **Tiny apple solids**
- **Proteins and pectin** that cause haze  

When fermentation slows, these particles lose buoyancy and begin to settle.  
Over time, gravity and chemistry work together to separate:
- Clear cider (top)
- Sediment, or **lees** (bottom)

---

## 🧪 2. **Natural Clarification (Gravity + Time)**

### Timeframe
- Usually takes **2–4 weeks** after racking.  
- In cool conditions (50–60 °F / 10–15 °C), clearing is faster and cleaner.

### Process
1. Leave the jug undisturbed in a cool, dark place.  
2. As yeast and proteins settle, a compact sediment layer forms.  
3. The cider above gradually becomes clear.  

💡 *Tip:* Shine a flashlight from the back or side — the difference between cloudy and clear zones becomes visible each day.

---

## 🧴 3. **Fining (Optional Acceleration)**
If your cider remains hazy after a month, you can use **fining agents** — natural clarifiers that bind and pull haze particles to the bottom.  

| Clarifier | Dose (per gallon) | Works best on | Notes |
|------------|------------------|----------------|--------|
| **Bentonite** | ½ tsp | Proteins | Mix in warm water, stir gently |
| **Gelatin** | ½ tsp | Yeast haze | Use cold cider, clarifies fast |
| **Sparkolloid** | 1 tsp | General haze | Excellent clarity, neutral taste |
| **Pectic enzyme** | ½ tsp | Pectin haze | Add early or after fermentation if cloudy |

Only use **one** at a time and always after fermentation is complete (SG ≤ 1.000).  

---

## 🔁 4. **Cold Crashing (Optional)**
To speed settling and preserve crispness:

1. Place cider in a refrigerator or cold area (35–40 °F / 1–4 °C).  
2. Leave it 2–3 days — yeast drops out rapidly.  
3. Rack off the clear cider gently afterward.

---

## 💧 5. **Racking for Final Clarity**

Once clear:
1. Sanitize your siphon and a new jug.  
2. Siphon slowly, leaving sediment behind.  
3. Avoid splashing — oxygen can dull flavor and color.  

Repeat if necessary for brilliant clarity before bottling.

---

## 🥂 6. **Result**
A well-clarified cider will be:
- Visually transparent, bright, and golden.  
- Clean on the palate — crisp apple flavor without yeasty residue.  
- Stable for aging or carbonation.  

---

## ⚠️ Professional Notes
- Never add clarifiers until fermentation is **completely finished**.  
- Don’t disturb or shake the jug during clarification.  
- Patience always yields the clearest and most stable cider.

## ✅ Step-by-Step: Post-Fermentation Actions

### 1️⃣ Confirm Fermentation Is Finished
Before bottling or aging, **verify stability**:
- Use a **hydrometer** if possible.  
  - **Final Gravity (FG)** should be **≤ 1.000**.  
  - Take two readings, 24 h apart — they must be identical.  
  - If it’s stable and dry, fermentation is complete.  

If you don’t have a hydrometer but there’s **zero bubbling, no sediment movement, and no pressure**, that’s also a strong visual confirmation.

---

### 2️⃣ Taste and Evaluate
Pour a small sample:
- **Aroma:** clean apple, no sulfur, no vinegar.  
- **Taste:** crisp, balanced, slightly dry (since yeast consumed the sugar).  
If it’s good now, it will age beautifully.  

---

### 3️⃣ Decide the Finish You Want  

#### ➤ **A. Still (non-sparkling) cider**
1. Rack carefully into sanitized bottles, avoiding sediment.  
2. Cap or cork.  
3. Label and date them.  
4. **Age 2–4 weeks** in a cool, dark place (55–65 °F / 13–18 °C).  
5. Chill before serving.  

#### ➤ **B. Sparkling cider**
1. In a sanitized bottling bucket, dissolve **1 oz (28 g)** of sugar in **¼ cup hot water** and let it cool.  
2. Gently rack the cider onto this solution and stir very lightly.  
3. Bottle in **beer bottles** (never wine bottles).  
4. Cap and store at **70 °F (21 °C)** for **7–10 days** to carbonate.  
5. Then refrigerate all bottles to stop further fermentation.  

---

### 4️⃣ Optional — Back-Sweeten (if you want a smoother taste)
If you prefer a touch of sweetness:
1. Add **¼ tsp potassium sorbate + ¼ Campden tablet per gallon** to stabilize.  
2. Wait 24 h.  
3. Sweeten with apple juice concentrate, sugar, or honey to taste.  
4. Bottle as still cider (not sparkling, since sorbate blocks carbonation).  

---

### 5️⃣ Aging
- **Young cider (2–4 weeks)** → bright, crisp, and fresh.  
- **Aged cider (6–8 weeks)** → smoother, more complex, deeper apple aroma.  
Store bottles upright in a cool, dark location.

---

## ⚠️ Quick Checkpoints
- Make sure all equipment is **fully sanitized** before bottling.  
- If bottling sparkling cider, never exceed 1 oz sugar per gallon — excess pressure = bottle bombs.  
- Always label bottles with **date + type** (e.g., “Golden Dry Cider – Batch 1”).
