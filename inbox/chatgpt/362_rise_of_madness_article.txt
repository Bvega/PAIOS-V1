# The Rise of Madness

In the past five years, the world has witnessed a whirlwind of political chaos, geopolitical upheaval, and even religious transformation that few could have anticipated. From Washington to Warsaw and from Tehran to the Vatican, seismic shifts have upended norms and left societies questioning their beliefs and direction. **“The Rise of Madness”** chronicles this tumultuous period – examining how divisive U.S. politics, conflicts in Europe and the Middle East, and the unprecedented ascension of an American pope are together reshaping public thought, behavior, and ideology across the globe. The tone of this journey is reflective and critical, yet firmly grounded in fact and evidence.

## United States – A House Divided Against Itself

No arena has embodied the recent turbulence more than American politics. During Donald Trump’s presidency (2017-2021), the United States became a stage for extraordinary division and rage. Trump’s populist brand of politics – characterized by fiery rhetoric, norm-defying behavior, and an **“us vs. them”** ethos – both energized and polarized the nation. His final year in office was marked by unprecedented turmoil: a bitterly contested 2020 election, rampant misinformation about voter fraud, and the shocking insurrection at the U.S. Capitol on January 6, 2021, when a mob of his supporters stormed Congress in an effort to overturn the election results【34†L59-L67】【18†L430-L437】. The attack, which many Americans viewed as an **“attack on democracy”**, underscored just how deeply fractured the country had become – about half of Americans labeled January 6 an insurrection that threatened democracy, while a sizable minority insisted it was either a lawful protest or time to simply move on【18†L430-L437】. The very fact that the definition of an attempted insurrection was up for debate illustrated the **national schism** in truth and perspective.

Under Trump, partisan loyalties hardened into identities. Even after he left office, the **“Big Lie”** – the unfounded belief that the 2020 election was stolen – persisted, embraced by a majority of Republicans and fueled by conspiracy theories like QAnon【34†L59-L67】【34†L79-L87】. This poisoned well of misinformation eroded trust in democratic institutions and contributed to what Freedom House described as a precipitous decline in U.S. democratic standards【34†L59-L67】. By 2023, a Pew Research study found Americans’ views of politics to be *“unrelentingly negative,”* with only 4% saying the political system was working well and 63% expressing little to no confidence in the future of U.S. democracy【33†L188-L194】. Cynicism and exhaustion reigned – two-thirds of Americans admitted they felt *“exhausted”* when thinking about politics【33†L132-L140】. Voter turnout hit historic highs in 2018, 2020, and 2022, yet this civic energy coexisted with record-low trust in government【33†L179-L187】【33†L188-L194】. In short, the U.S. became a **house divided**: highly engaged in the political process, but deeply disillusioned by it.

After Trump’s presidency, President Joe Biden sought to restore a sense of normalcy and unity. His administration rejoined global agreements (like the Paris climate accord), promoted COVID-19 vaccinations, and pursued bipartisan infrastructure spending at home. Yet the tide of division did not simply recede. Cultural flashpoints – from pandemic mask mandates and school curricula to Supreme Court battles over abortion – kept the public square hot. The hardline **“Make America Great Again”** (MAGA) movement remained a potent force, with Trump himself relentlessly stoking grievances from the sidelines. Indeed, in a dramatic turn of events, Trump mounted a political comeback in 2024. Capitalizing on economic anxieties and a perception among his followers that traditional politicians had failed them, Trump won the 2024 presidential election, defeating the incumbent Vice President and becoming only the second U.S. president in history to serve non-consecutive terms【39†L84-L92】. His return to the White House on January 20, 2025, proved that the populist fervor he ignited was no fleeting phenomenon – it had become an enduring feature of American life【39†L84-L92】. 

This second act of Trump’s presidency has only deepened the *“madness”* in the American political psyche. Supporters see vindication and a chance to *“finish the job”* of uprooting the establishment, while opponents see a democracy on the brink. The partisan information silos that define American discourse have grown even more impermeable. By early 2025, even monumental non-political events were filtered through this divide – for instance, when the Vatican announced the election of a new pope in May, President Trump took to his own social media platform to hail it as a *“Great Honor for our Country,”* implicitly claiming a share of the moment for the United States【10†L153-L161】. It was an oddly revealing gesture: even a sacred religious event was spun into a point of national pride in the ongoing culture wars. In America’s house divided, every occurrence – whether political, cultural or religious – becomes another battleground for warring ideologies.

## Europe – Geopolitical Earthquakes and Nationalist Tremors

Across the Atlantic, Europe has weathered its own storms of upheaval that have dramatically reordered the continent’s politics and security. The past five years have felt like history on fast-forward for Europeans: a pandemic, a ground war, an energy crisis, and a resurgent wave of nationalism – all colliding to test the European project’s cohesion and values.

The first tremors came with **Brexit** formally taking effect in early 2020. The United Kingdom’s departure from the EU, driven by nationalist and anti-establishment sentiment, sent a jolt through Europe’s political order. While Brexit’s immediate dramas eventually subsided, it foreshadowed battles to come over identity, sovereignty, and the future of globalization. Only two years later, a far greater earthquake struck: **Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022**. This was the largest military attack on European soil since World War II – a **geopolitical cataclysm** that shattered decades of relative peace on the continent【45†L219-L226】. Within days, the unthinkable had happened: Kyiv under bombardment, millions of Ukrainians fleeing westward as refugees, and European leaders scrambling to respond to a brutal war at their doorstep.

Russia’s aggression paradoxically galvanized Europe into newfound unity – at least initially. In the face of Vladimir Putin’s onslaught, the European Union and NATO moved with surprising speed and unanimity. Historic shifts ensued. Neutral countries Sweden and Finland, shocked out of complacency by Moscow’s threat, abandoned decades of non-alignment and applied to join NATO in 2022; Finland became a member by April 2023, and Sweden followed in March 2024【41†L43-L51】【41†L47-L55】. Europe’s collective defense, long taken for granted, suddenly felt urgent and real. Meanwhile, the EU imposed wave after wave of sanctions on Russia and committed billions of euros in aid and arms to Ukraine. “*Russia’s invasion of Ukraine is transforming Europe profoundly*,” observed analysts, noting that the war revitalized the NATO alliance and forced the EU to confront hard power politics once again【30†L21-L29】【41†L43-L51】.

Yet unity had its limits. As the war ground on into 2023 and 2024, the strain on Europe grew. Russia weaponized its energy exports in retaliation, cutting off gas supplies and triggering a painful energy crisis. Europeans endured spiking fuel prices and inflation as the cost of standing up to Moscow【22†L1-L9】. By the second winter of war, cracks in the solidarity began to show. Populist and far-right parties across Europe exploited war fatigue and economic anxieties, arguing that ordinary Europeans were paying the price for others’ battles. A **nationalist backlash** gathered steam, feeding off not only war issues but also long-standing grievances over immigration and cultural change. In country after country, from Italy to Sweden to Hungary, right-wing populist forces either took power or gained ground by railing against immigrants, the LGBTQ community, feminism, and even support for Ukraine【32†L75-L83】【32†L79-L87】. As one analysis put it, *“a backlash against immigration, LGBTQ rights, abortion and support for Ukraine is unfolding across the continent,”* buoying parties that brand themselves as protectors of the *“real”* Europe【32†L75-L83】【32†L79-L87】. 

This trend came to a head in late 2023. In Italy, Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni – a hard-right nationalist – fulminated against African migrants arriving by boat. In Poland and Slovakia, election campaigns fanned fears of foreign influence and *“Ukraine fatigue.”* Indeed, Slovaks elected a populist leader who promptly cut off military aid to Ukraine, breaking ranks with EU policy【32†L105-L114】【32†L110-L118】. Such developments raised the specter of Europe’s unity fracturing from within. The **war in Ukraine**, which initially united Europe in defense of democratic values, also became a catalyst for the **rise of Europe’s far-right**, who capitalized on public frustration over economic hardship and social change. 

Despite these internal rifts, Europe’s core project has thus far survived the madness. The EU remains intact (if a bit bruised), and NATO has only grown stronger and larger. Crucially, Ukraine’s fate hangs in the balance of European resolve. By 2025, the war still rages in the Donbas and along the Dnipro, testing whether Europe’s commitment can outlast Russia’s aggression. The conflict has already **“unsettled European energy security”** and contributed to higher food prices globally【21†L1-L9】. It has also reminded Europeans of the fragility of peace – a *“harshness of the world”* that many had comfortably forgotten【30†L31-L39】. In response, Europe has had to reawaken old instincts: taking refugees in on a massive scale, boosting defense spending, and confronting nationalist demons of its own.

In sum, Europe’s last five years have been defined by **geopolitical earthquakes** – war, energy shocks, and realignment – and the **tremors of nationalism** they’ve unleashed. This volatile mix is reshaping European public opinion. Some citizens have rallied around democratic ideals and solidarity with Ukraine, while others have gravitated to insular nationalist messages, feeling overwhelmed by refugees or disillusioned by economic strain. The *“madness”* in Europe lies in this dueling trajectory: a continent pulled between unity and fracture, between its liberal post-war identity and illiberal currents that threaten to turn back the clock. As Europe stands at this crossroads, the choices it makes will echo far beyond its borders.

## Middle East – Shifting Sands and Divine Surprises

The Middle East, already long synonymous with volatility, has seen **old enmities give way to new alliances** – and vice versa – in a dizzying dance of geopolitical realignment these past five years. The region’s political landscape in 2020 looked vastly different from that of 2025, marked by landmark peace deals, devastating wars, and an astonishing reshuffle of great-power influence. Amid these changes, religious and ideological currents in the Middle East have also been roiled, influencing how societies see themselves and the world.

One of the most significant developments was the signing of the **Abraham Accords** in late 2020. Brokered by the Trump administration, these U.S.-backed agreements normalized relations between Israel and several Arab states – notably the United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, and later Morocco【24†L103-L112】【24†L105-L113】. For a region where the Arab-Israeli conflict had defined alliances for generations, the Accords were a geopolitical earthquake of a different kind: a hopeful one. Arab monarchies and Israel, once sworn foes, openly embraced economic and security cooperation, driven by a shared view of Iran as a looming threat and mutual interests in technology and trade【24†L111-L119】. The Accords signaled a paradigm shift – *“a significant geopolitical shift in the Middle East”* – suggesting that pragmatic interests could overcome old prejudices【23†L23-L31】【24†L103-L112】. Many spoke of a *“new Middle East”* dawning, as erstwhile enemies shook hands in photo-ops that would have been unthinkable a few years prior.

However, this new regional harmony would soon be tested by the enduring powder keg of the **Israel-Palestinian conflict**. In October 2023, violence exploded once more when Hamas militants from Gaza carried out a shocking large-scale attack on Israel, prompting a devastating Israeli military response and war in Gaza. The Israel-Hamas war that ensued was one of the bloodiest in the conflict’s history, killing thousands of civilians in Gaza and forcing a re-examination of regional relationships. Arab states that had signed peace deals with Israel faced intense public pressure to hold Israel accountable for Palestinian suffering. The much-anticipated next step of the Abraham Accords – a potential normalization between Israel and Saudi Arabia – was abruptly put on ice as the Saudi kingdom froze talks in the wake of the Gaza war【26†L1-L9】. Still, tellingly, the core Abraham Accords survived this crisis: UAE, Bahrain, and others condemned Israeli tactics but did not sever ties. As one analysis noted in early 2025, *“the Arab-Israeli agreements have largely survived the war”* in Gaza, though their future hinges on broader regional dynamics and whether Israel addresses Palestinian aspirations【25†L1-L9】. In short, the **new friendships** in the Middle East proved resilient, but **old wounds** remained raw and far from healed.

At the same time, a parallel realignment was underway involving the region’s other powers. **American influence, long preeminent in the Middle East, began to wane**, creating space for new actors. The U.S. military’s chaotic withdrawal from Afghanistan in August 2021 – ending a 20-year war and leaving the Taliban back in power – sent a troubling signal to allies about American staying power. Traditional U.S. partners like Saudi Arabia and the Gulf states started hedging their bets, seeking arrangements that did not rely solely on Washington. In a stunning development, **Saudi Arabia and Iran – Sunni and Shia archrivals – agreed to restore diplomatic ties in 2023 in a deal brokered by China**【26†L29-L37】. This Chinese-mediated détente between Riyadh and Tehran caught many observers off-guard and underscored Beijing’s rising profile in the region at America’s expense. The Saudi-Iran rapprochement eased one of the Middle East’s most dangerous feuds (cooling proxy conflicts in Yemen and elsewhere) and reduced the incentive for Arab states to unite with Israel against Iran【26†L29-L37】. For Saudi Arabia, the deal was part of a broader strategy: diversify great-power partnerships. Over the past five years, the Saudi leadership under Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman has courted Chinese investment and even joined China-led institutions, while also engaging with Russia – all while keeping one foot in the U.S. camp【26†L49-L58】【26†L55-L63】. The **“unipolar moment”** of uncontested American dominance in the Middle East has visibly faded; a more multipolar, transactional order has emerged, where Gulf monarchies feel free to pursue *“multiple alignments as a long-term hedge”*【26†L49-L58】.

The Middle East’s shifting sands have also included the slow rehabilitation of pariah regimes and the cooling of conflicts. In Syria, President Bashar al-Assad, once ostracized for his brutality in the civil war, has been steadily welcomed back into the Arab diplomatic fold – Syria was readmitted to the Arab League in 2023, symbolizing how priorities shifted from ideals to realpolitik. In Yemen, a brutal proxy war between Saudi- and Iran-aligned forces eased, with ceasefires in 2022 and tentative peace talks by 2024 offering hope that one of the world’s worst humanitarian crises might abate. Even Turkey, straddling Europe and the Middle East, played both sides to its advantage: mediating between Ukraine and Russia in one moment, while conducting military incursions against Kurdish forces in another. In summary, the geopolitical kaleidoscope of the Middle East has been turning rapidly, producing new patterns of alliance and rivalry. 

Alongside these political shifts, **the region’s social and religious currents have been no less turbulent**. A striking example came from within the Islamic Republic of Iran. In September 2022, a 22-year-old woman named Mahsa Amini died in custody after being arrested by Iran’s morality police for allegedly violating strict hijab rules. Her death ignited an unprecedented wave of protests across Iran, led largely by women and youth, under the banner *“Woman, Life, Freedom.”* The regime cracked down harshly – over 500 protesters were killed and tens of thousands arrested【36†L112-L120】 – yet the uprising persisted for months, morphing into a broad challenge to the hardline theocracy. While the protests eventually ebbed under repression, they marked a **cultural paradigm shift** in Iran that is still rippling through society【36†L123-L126】. Never before had Iran’s post-1979 Islamic regime faced such widespread, sustained dissent from its own people demanding personal freedoms and an end to clerical rule. The **ideological gulf** between an aging authoritarian elite and a young, globally connected populace is widening in Iran and in many Middle Eastern societies. In other countries, too, old dogmas are being questioned: from Saudi women cautiously enjoying new freedoms (like driving and public entertainment) as the kingdom modernizes, to younger Arabs expressing fatigue with sectarian divisions. Public thought and behavior are evolving, even if change is uneven and often suppressed.

## Faith and Fury – An American Pope and a World Transformed

In a final twist to this global saga, the past five years have also seen history made in the realm of religion – with echoes that resonate in society and politics. For the first time in its 2,000-year history, the Roman Catholic Church elected **an American pope**. On May 8, 2025, white smoke rose above the Sistine Chapel in Vatican City, signaling that the College of Cardinals had chosen a new pontiff. The announcement stunned many: **Cardinal Robert Francis Prevost of Chicago** would become Pope Leo XIV, the first ever pope from the United States【3†L33-L38】. This watershed moment in the Catholic Church carried deep significance. The papacy had always remained a largely European (often Italian) institution, in part due to an unspoken **“taboo against a U.S. pontiff”** – a concern that a superpower nation’s pope might unduly politicize the spiritual office【13†L142-L150】【13†L147-L155】. Prevost had to *“overcome the taboo”* of America’s geopolitical might to ascend to the throne of St. Peter【13†L147-L155】. That the cardinals ultimately broke with tradition speaks to the dynamic changes within the Church and the world at large.

Pope Leo XIV (Prevost) brings with him a unique background and a set of expectations colored by the turbulent era of his election. At 69, this American cleric had spent much of his career as a missionary bishop in Peru, straddling North and South, conservative and progressive currents. In fact, he was a protégé of Pope Francis: Francis brought Prevost to the Vatican in 2023 to oversee the powerful office that appoints bishops【13†L142-L150】, and elevated him to Cardinal in early 2025 as if preparing him for larger responsibilities【13†L169-L177】. In many ways, Leo XIV’s election can be seen as a referendum on Pope Francis’s legacy. Francis’s 12-year papacy (2013-2025) was transformative – he championed mercy over dogma, spoke out on climate change, welcomed migrants, and urged a more inclusive church (famously asking, *“Who am I to judge?”* regarding gay Catholics)【3†L51-L60】【3†L61-L69】. This agenda thrilled progressives but angered traditionalists, creating a rift within the global Church. The cardinals who gathered in conclave were thus at a crossroads: continue Francis’s path of reform or revert to a more conservative, doctrinal course【3†L69-L77】.

In choosing an American with a reputation as a level-headed administrator and bridge-builder, the Church signaled a desire for unity amid division. **Pope Leo XIV’s first words** to the crowd from St. Peter’s balcony were telling: *“Peace be with you… so we can all walk together,”* he said, stressing dialogue *“without fear”*【10†L175-L183】. Speaking in both Italian and Spanish (a nod to his years in Latin America), the new pope presented himself as a healer and unifier. His very identity as an American could have complex ripple effects. On one hand, it thrilled U.S. Catholics to see one of their own in the Vatican’s highest office – even President Trump gushed with congratulatory pride at *“the first American Pope…What excitement, and what a Great Honor for our Country,”* in a message on Truth Social【10†L155-L163】【10†L158-L161】. For a politically polarized American Catholic community – split between progressives aligned with Pope Francis and a vocal contingent of **“radical traditionalist”** or even **“MAGA” Catholics** who found Francis too liberal – the impact of a compatriot pope is profound. Some U.S. conservatives hope Pope Leo XIV will roll back what they saw as Francis’s excesses, while others note that Prevost was actually a supporter of Francis’s inclusionary moves (he even presided over a **revolutionary reform adding women to the committee that vets bishops**【13†L169-L177】). The direction Leo XIV will take remains to be seen, but there is no doubt that the Catholic Church – like the world – is at a crossroads between tradition and change.

Globally, a pope from the United States holds a mirror to America’s complicated role in the world. As one Vatican historian observed on the eve of the conclave, few figures can *“move opinion, drive conversation, and call for change like a pope,”* and the choice of pope can either reinforce or redirect the **“moral compass”** of world affairs【3†L51-L59】【3†L79-L87】. Will Pope Leo XIV, coming from the wealthiest nation on earth, focus the Church on social justice and the **“unity of the human family”**, or will he cater more to doctrinal purists?【3†L79-L87】 His election comes at a tense time: conflicts rage (from Ukraine to Gaza), nationalism and anti-migrant sentiment are on the rise, and even the concept of truth is often under assault. In this context, the moral voice of the papacy carries weight. That voice now has a distinctly American accent, for better or worse. It is unprecedented territory – and for many Catholics and non-Catholics alike, it prompts reflection on the intersection of faith and worldly power.

## Conclusion – A World Rebalanced by Chaos

From the spectacle of a U.S. president egging on an insurrection, to tanks rolling across European plains, to diplomats in flowing robes signing peace deals in the desert, to an American bishop donning the white papal cassock – the last five years have often felt surreal. The **“Rise of Madness”** is an attempt to make sense of this turbulence, and to trace the threads that connect these disparate events. What emerges is a portrait of a world in flux, where old certainties have crumbled and new paradigms are struggling to be born.

Common themes run through these stories. **Polarization and identity politics** – whether national, ideological, or religious – have pulled communities apart in the U.S., Europe, and the Middle East alike. The quest for **“strongman”** leaders or nostalgic certitude has gained appeal in all these regions (think of Trump’s comeback, Europe’s nationalist surge, or various Middle Eastern autocrats tightening their grip), often as a fearful reaction against a fast-changing world. Meanwhile, **globalization’s unravelling** has been hastened by conflict and pandemic: supply chains frayed, borders hardened, and a sense of vulnerability to distant shocks (a war in Ukraine, a virus from Wuhan) has permeated public consciousness【20†L909-L918】【20†L895-L903】. In response, many people have turned inward to tribe, sect, or nation, seeking solace in narrower identities. This inward turn has been turbocharged by social media echo chambers that amplify outrage and conspiracy, making it ever harder to find common ground on truth.

And yet, even amid the madness, **counter-currents of hope and transformation** flow. The shock of war in Europe reawakened democratic solidarity and reminded people what’s at stake in defending freedom. In the Middle East, ancient foes shaking hands and women defiantly cutting their hair both hint at the possibility of change long deemed impossible. In the Catholic Church, the elevation of a once-improbable leader from the New World speaks to an institution striving to broaden its embrace. These developments influence public ideology in subtler ways: by expanding the imagination of what can change. For every disillusioned voter or angry protester in the streets, there is another citizen quietly reevaluating their beliefs as they watch the world’s drama unfold. Are the old left-right political binaries adequate to solve today’s crises? How much of one’s identity should be rooted in nation or religion? What values are worth defending when *“democracy [comes] under siege”*【66†L23-L31】? Such questions are being asked in living rooms and coffee shops from Middle America to the Middle East.

In this era of upheaval, one lesson stands out: **the interconnection of all these shifts**. A populist wave in America can embolden nationalists in Europe【19†L37-L40】. A war in Europe can reshape alliances in the Middle East【26†L29-L37】. A decision in the Vatican can send ripples into politics (as seen when Pope Francis’s stance on migrants and climate drew ire from nationalist leaders). The world’s ideological and political ecosystems are linked as never before, for better and worse. We are, to quote Pope Francis, *“all walking together”* on a shared journey【10†L179-L187】 – even if that journey lately feels like a stumble through chaos.

**The past five years have indeed felt like a rise of madness**, but they have also been a test of resilience and values. How we respond – whether with wisdom or with more fury – will determine the future course of this still-unfolding story. As the smoke (white or otherwise) clears from the tumult of recent events, humanity faces a choice: to be consumed by the madness we have unleashed, or to learn from it and steer our world toward a saner, more just equilibrium. The answer will shape politics, geopolitics, religion, and our very souls in the years to come.

## Trump Returns Amid Polarized Electorate

After a bitterly contested election cycle, Donald J. Trump was sworn in this January as the 47th President of the United States, defeating Vice President Kamala Harris and becoming only the second U.S. leader in history to serve non-consecutive terms.

Trump's victory followed a tumultuous period of political division. His first term, from 2017 to 2021, culminated in the Jan. 6 Capitol riot and a second impeachment. Yet, his base remained resolute, mobilizing around economic anxieties, cultural grievances, and what he repeatedly termed the “corrupt Washington elite.”

According to Pew Research, confidence in American democratic institutions has reached record lows, with only 4% of Americans stating the system is functioning well. Political engagement remains high, but so does exhaustion and distrust.

“American politics today is a war of identity,” said Dr. Karen Meyers, a political scientist at Georgetown University. “It’s no longer just policy—it’s about who belongs and who decides the rules.”

## War and Realignment in Europe

In February 2022, Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine launched Europe into its most significant security crisis since World War II. The war has since reshaped NATO, pushed Finland and Sweden into the alliance, and exposed Europe’s dependence on Russian energy.

“The invasion fundamentally changed Europe,” said Josep Borrell, EU foreign affairs chief. “We woke up from a geopolitical slumber.”

But the war also intensified political divides. While initial unity gave way to sanctions and support for Ukraine, nationalist movements across Europe gained momentum. Right-wing leaders from Italy to Slovakia criticized migration policies and warned against “war fatigue.”

In Poland, Hungary, and France, hard-right parties have made significant electoral gains, often using cultural grievances and economic hardship as campaign fuel.

## Middle East in Motion

In the Middle East, once-unthinkable alliances have emerged. The Abraham Accords normalized relations between Israel and Arab nations like the UAE, Bahrain, and Morocco. However, the October 2023 Israel-Hamas war strained those agreements, especially as civilian casualties in Gaza drew criticism across the Arab world.

Further shifting the region’s landscape, Iran and Saudi Arabia restored diplomatic relations in 2023 through a China-brokered agreement. The deal marked a rare détente between Sunni and Shia powers, marginalizing American influence and highlighting Beijing’s growing diplomatic reach.

Meanwhile, Iran saw its largest protests in decades after the death of Mahsa Amini in police custody. The ensuing “Woman, Life, Freedom” movement was met with brutal crackdowns but ignited a generational rift that persists.

## A New Pope, From America

In a landmark decision on May 8, the College of Cardinals elected Robert Francis Prevost of Chicago as Pope Leo XIV, making him the first American to lead the Roman Catholic Church.

The decision ends a centuries-long unofficial tradition of European dominance over the papacy and comes at a time of theological and political tension within the Church. Prevost, a former missionary in Peru and ally of Pope Francis, is seen as a moderate bridge-builder.

“His appointment is symbolically powerful,” said Rev. Thomas Reese, a Vatican analyst. “But it also opens questions about U.S. influence on global Catholicism.”

President Trump praised the election on his Truth Social platform as a “Great Honor for our Country,” prompting criticism from religious scholars who warned against politicizing the papacy.

## A Global Reckoning

The convergence of these events—political polarization in the U.S., war and nationalism in Europe, shifting power dynamics in the Middle East, and a religious transformation in the Vatican—underscores a broader global reckoning.

Institutions once seen as unshakable—liberal democracy, Western alliances, even the Church—are being tested by movements that reject globalism, challenge elite authority, and demand a return to more insular values.

“It’s not just politics,” said Anne-Marie Slaughter, CEO of New America. “It’s a psychological and cultural revolt. People are trying to reclaim control in a world that feels increasingly chaotic.”

The path forward remains unclear. But as nationalism, identity politics, and moral realignments continue to reshape the global order, one thing is certain: the world is no longer what it was five years ago.
