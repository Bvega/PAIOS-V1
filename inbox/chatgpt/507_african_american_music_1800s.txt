What music did African American played on the late 1800’s

In the late 1800s, African Americans played a variety of musical styles that laid the foundation for modern American music. Some of the most prominent forms included:

1. **Spirituals** – Rooted in African rhythms and Christian influences, spirituals were religious folk songs that expressed faith, struggle, and hope. These songs often used call-and-response and were sung in churches and during labor.

2. **Work Songs and Field Hollers** – These were rhythmic songs sung by enslaved and freed African Americans while working in fields, railroads, or prisons. They featured strong, repetitive rhythms and call-and-response patterns.

3. **Blues (Early Forms)** – Though blues as a distinct genre developed in the early 20th century, its roots were forming in the late 1800s. Influenced by spirituals and work songs, early blues was characterized by sorrowful lyrics and expressive melodies.

4. **Ragtime** – Emerging in the late 19th century, ragtime featured syncopated (offbeat) rhythms and was popularized by African American pianists like Scott Joplin. It became widely influential and was a precursor to jazz.

5. **Brass Band Music** – Many African Americans played in brass bands, especially after the Civil War when military instruments became more available. These bands performed marches, polkas, and dance music, influencing early jazz.

6. **Minstrel Music (African American Participation)** – Though minstrel shows often portrayed racist stereotypes, some Black musicians performed in these shows and introduced authentic Black musical traditions to wider audiences.

7. **Folk Music and Banjo Traditions** – The banjo, which has African origins, was commonly used in African American folk music. Black musicians played dance tunes, ballads, and instrumental pieces that influenced later country and blues music.

These styles not only defined the music of the late 19th century but also provided the foundation for jazz, gospel, and rhythm and blues in the 20th century.
