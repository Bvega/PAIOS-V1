To make 1 gallon of hard cider at home, you'll need some basic ingredients and equipment. Here's a simple step-by-step guide to help you brew your own hard cider:

### Ingredients:
1. **Apple Juice (1 gallon):** Use preservative-free apple juice (look for "100% apple juice" with no added preservatives like potassium sorbate or sodium benzoate, as these will prevent fermentation).
2. **Sugar (Optional):** Adding sugar can increase the alcohol content. Cane sugar, brown sugar, or honey are common choices. Use about 1/2 to 1 cup depending on your preference.
3. **Yeast:** Use cider yeast, wine yeast (like Lalvin EC-1118), or even ale yeast (like Safale S-04). The type of yeast will affect the final flavor and characteristics.
4. **Yeast Nutrient (Optional):** Helps fermentation start strong (about 1/2 tsp).
5. **Campden Tablet (Optional):** To sanitize the juice before fermentation. Use 1 tablet per gallon if needed.

### Equipment:
1. **Fermenter:** A 1-gallon glass carboy or food-safe plastic container with an airlock.
2. **Airlock and Bung:** Allows CO2 to escape while keeping oxygen and contaminants out.
3. **Hydrometer (Optional):** For measuring the starting and final gravity to calculate alcohol content.
4. **Siphoning Tube:** For transferring cider without disturbing the sediment.
5. **Sanitizer:** Star San or similar, to sanitize all equipment.

### Instructions:

#### Step 1: Sanitize Equipment
- Thoroughly clean and sanitize all equipment that will come into contact with the juice. This includes the fermenter, airlock, siphoning tube, and any utensils. Contamination can ruin your cider.

#### Step 2: Prepare the Juice
- Pour the apple juice into your sanitized fermenter. Leave some headspace (about 2-3 inches from the top) to allow for fermentation activity.

#### Step 3: Add Sugar (Optional)
- If you want a higher alcohol content, dissolve the sugar in a small amount of juice or water and then add it to the fermenter. Stir gently to mix.

#### Step 4: Add Yeast Nutrient (Optional)
- If using yeast nutrient, add it now according to the package instructions. This step can help ensure a strong fermentation.

#### Step 5: Pitch the Yeast
- Sprinkle the yeast directly into the fermenter. Alternatively, you can rehydrate the yeast according to the manufacturer's instructions before adding it to the juice. Stir gently to mix the yeast into the juice.

#### Step 6: Install the Airlock
- Attach the airlock to the top of the fermenter and fill it with a small amount of sanitized water or vodka. This will allow CO2 to escape while preventing any unwanted bacteria or wild yeast from entering.

#### Step 7: Fermentation
- Place the fermenter in a dark, cool place (60-75°F or 15-24°C) for 1-2 weeks. You'll notice bubbling in the airlock, which indicates fermentation is happening. When bubbling slows down significantly or stops, primary fermentation is complete.

#### Step 8: Rack and Secondary Fermentation (Optional)
- For a clearer cider, you can siphon (rack) the cider into a second sanitized fermenter, leaving the sediment (lees) behind. Attach the airlock again and allow it to ferment for an additional 1-2 weeks.

#### Step 9: Bottling
- Once fermentation is complete and the cider is clear, you can bottle it. If you want carbonated cider, add 1/2 teaspoon of priming sugar per 12 oz bottle before filling and capping. Be sure to use bottles that can withstand carbonation pressure, like beer bottles or plastic soda bottles.

#### Step 10: Aging and Carbonation
- Store the bottles at room temperature for 1-2 weeks to allow carbonation to develop. After this, move them to a cool place (like a refrigerator) and let them age for at least another week. Cider tends to taste better after a month or more of aging.

#### Step 11: Enjoy!
- Chill your bottles in the fridge, pour into a glass, and enjoy your homemade hard cider!

### Tips:
- Always taste your cider throughout the process to get a sense of how it changes.
- Adjust sugar levels and yeast choices based on the flavor profile and alcohol content you desire.

Here's a breakdown of the ingredients you have and how they can be used in your 1-gallon cider-making process:

### 1. **Campden Tablets**
- **What They Are:** Campden tablets contain potassium metabisulfite or sodium metabisulfite, which are used to sanitize and inhibit wild yeast and bacteria in juice.
- **How to Use:** Crush 1 Campden tablet per gallon of juice and dissolve it in your cider must (unfermented juice). Stir well and let it sit for 24 hours before adding yeast. This ensures any wild microorganisms are killed off, creating a clean environment for your chosen yeast strain.

### 2. **Pectic Enzyme**
- **What It Is:** Pectic enzyme breaks down pectin in fruit, which helps to prevent cloudiness (pectin haze) in your cider.
- **How to Use:** Add 1/2 teaspoon of pectic enzyme per gallon of juice at the same time you add the Campden tablet. This helps clarify the cider and releases more juice and flavor from the fruit. Let it sit for at least 12 hours before adding yeast.

### 3. **Wine Tannin Powder**
- **What It Is:** Tannin powder adds astringency and mouthfeel to cider, which can balance out the sweetness and add complexity.
- **How to Use:** Add 1/8 teaspoon of wine tannin per gallon of juice. Add it to the juice before fermentation to improve body and structure, especially if your cider tastes flat or lacks complexity.

### 4. **Ascorbic Acid**
- **What It Is:** Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) is an antioxidant that prevents oxidation, which can cause off-flavors and browning in cider.
- **How to Use:** Add 1/8 teaspoon of ascorbic acid per gallon of juice just before bottling. This helps preserve the fresh taste and color of the cider. It is not usually added before fermentation as it can affect yeast activity.

### 5. **Acid Blend**
- **What It Is:** Acid blend is a mixture of malic, citric, and tartaric acids that is used to adjust the acidity of cider, which can enhance flavor and balance.
- **How to Use:** Add 1/2 to 1 teaspoon per gallon, but it’s best to start with 1/4 teaspoon and taste the juice to avoid over-acidifying. You can add it before fermentation to adjust the juice’s acidity or after fermentation to fine-tune the flavor. Adjust based on taste and desired tartness.

### How to Incorporate These Additives into Your 1-Gallon Cider Process:

1. **Sanitize and Prepare Juice:**
   - Sanitize all equipment.
   - Crush and add 1 Campden tablet to the juice. Stir and let it sit for 24 hours.

2. **Add Pectic Enzyme:**
   - After 12 hours, add 1/2 teaspoon of pectic enzyme. Let sit for another 12 hours.

3. **Add Wine Tannin:**
   - If desired, add 1/8 teaspoon of wine tannin to improve body and complexity.

4. **Check Acidity and Add Acid Blend (Optional):**
   - Taste the juice and add acid blend incrementally (starting with 1/4 teaspoon) if more tartness or balance is needed.

5. **Fermentation:**
   - After 24 hours from adding the Campden tablet, pitch the yeast and attach the airlock.

6. **Post-Fermentation Adjustments:**
   - After fermentation is complete and before bottling, taste the cider. If needed, add more acid blend for balance.

7. **Add Ascorbic Acid Before Bottling:**
   - Just before bottling, add 1/8 teaspoon of ascorbic acid per gallon to prevent oxidation and maintain freshness.

This approach uses each additive to enhance your cider's flavor, clarity, and stability. Would you like more details on any specific step?

For a 1-gallon batch of hard cider, you typically need about **1/2 to 1 teaspoon** of dry yeast. Here's a quick guide based on yeast type:

- **Dry Wine or Cider Yeast (e.g., Lalvin EC-1118, Red Star Premier Blanc):** Use about 1/2 to 1 teaspoon. This amount is generally sufficient to ensure a good fermentation without over-pitching.
  
- **Ale Yeast (e.g., Safale S-04):** Use about 1/2 teaspoon. Ale yeasts can work well in cider, especially if you prefer a slightly fruitier profile.

- **Liquid Yeast:** For liquid yeast packs, you can use about 1/4 to 1/2 of the pack per gallon, as liquid yeasts have a higher cell count per volume compared to dry yeast.

Always ensure to rehydrate dry yeast according to the package instructions for the best results, or you can sprinkle it directly into the juice if preferred. Using the correct amount of yeast ensures proper fermentation without stressing the yeast or risking off-flavors.
