### Summary of the Briefing on the Dominican Republic – Robert F. Woodward Oral History (JFKOH-RTW-01)

Robert F. Woodward, Assistant Secretary of State for Inter-American Affairs under President Kennedy, gave a detailed account of the U.S. response to the Dominican Republic crisis following the assassination of dictator Rafael Trujillo in May 1961.

#### Key Points:

1. **Initial U.S. Uncertainty:**
   - After Trujillo’s assassination, it was unclear whether his son Ramfis Trujillo would continue the dictatorship or transition toward a representative government.
   - U.S. Chargé d’Affaires John C. Hill maintained close contacts across political groups to assess the situation.

2. **U.S. Strategy:**
   - President Kennedy was highly engaged in Dominican affairs.
   - Former Ambassador Robert Murphy was sent to evaluate potential U.S. support for democratic reforms.
   - The U.S. considered lifting the OAS (Organization of American States) embargo to encourage reform.

3. **Crisis and Naval Deployment:**
   - In November 1961, the situation deteriorated amid fears of repression.
   - U.S. Navy ships with Marines were stationed just outside Dominican territorial waters as a precaution.
   - Secretary of State Dean Rusk reviewed possible landing points in case military intervention was needed.

4. **Trujillo Family Departure:**
   - On November 18, 1961, Ramfis Trujillo unexpectedly exhumed his father's body, executed prisoners suspected of involvement in the assassination, and fled to Europe with family and wealth.
   - The U.S. facilitated the exit of other Trujillo family members, including ex-President Hector Trujillo, flying them to Fort Lauderdale with the understanding they’d soon leave the U.S.

5. **Outcome and Legacy:**
   - The crisis passed without U.S. intervention.
   - Woodward believed the Trujillos’ departure ultimately helped the Dominican Republic’s long-term stability.
   - The U.S. approach balanced non-intervention with strong readiness and diplomatic leverage.

This summary reflects a pivotal Cold War-era moment when the U.S. navigated between democratic ideals and regional stability, avoiding military involvement while actively shaping outcomes through diplomatic and covert support【11†source】.

### **Geo-Political Briefing & Analysis: Dominican Republic Crisis (1961–1962)**
**Source:** Robert F. Woodward Oral History Interview – January 8, 1970 (JFKOH-RTW-01)

---

### **I. Context & Background**
- **Event:** Assassination of Dominican dictator **Rafael Trujillo** (May 1961), ending a brutal 31-year regime.
- **Key Challenge:** Uncertainty over whether his son **Ramfis Trujillo** would continue authoritarian rule or facilitate democratic reform.
- **Geo-Political Climate:**  
   - **Cold War Era**: Heightened U.S. sensitivity to Soviet influence in Latin America.
   - **Cuba** had recently fallen to communism (1959) and the Bay of Pigs Invasion (1961) had just failed, escalating U.S. concerns over regional instability.
   - U.S. was promoting **“Alliance for Progress”**, aimed at supporting economic development and democratic institutions in Latin America to counteract leftist ideologies.

---

### **II. U.S. Strategy and Decisions**
1. **Diplomatic Observation & Assessment**
   - U.S. envoy **John C. Hill** maintained close relationships across Dominican factions to monitor political sentiment and stability.
   - **Robert Murphy**, former Ambassador, was dispatched by JFK to explore potential for a democratic transition.

2. **Kennedy’s Involvement**
   - President **John F. Kennedy** took personal interest in the Dominican situation.
   - The Dominican Republic was the first Latin American topic discussed at length by JFK with Woodward after the latter became Assistant Secretary for Inter-American Affairs.

3. **Intervention Readiness**
   - As the situation threatened to spiral into violence, the U.S. deployed **naval vessels and Marines** just outside Dominican waters, signaling readiness for intervention.
   - Secretary of State **Dean Rusk** personally reviewed maps of **Ciudad Trujillo** for potential marine landing points.

4. **Calculated Non-Intervention**
   - The U.S. did **not land troops**, avoiding the first break from its **non-intervention doctrine** in Latin America since 1931.
   - This restraint preserved diplomatic credibility while maintaining military deterrence.

5. **Facilitating Trujillo Family Exile**
   - **Ramfis Trujillo** exhumed his father's body, executed alleged assassins, and fled to Europe.
   - U.S. facilitated the departure of **Héctor Trujillo** and family to **Fort Lauderdale**, under the condition they would resettle elsewhere.

---

### **III. Geo-Political Implications**

#### **1. Regional Stability & U.S. Influence**
- The U.S. **prevented a potential civil war or repressive backlash** by pressuring the Trujillo regime diplomatically and preparing for intervention.
- Avoiding military deployment **strengthened U.S. credibility** among Latin American republics already wary of “Yankee imperialism.”

#### **2. Inter-American Relations & the OAS**
- The U.S. attempted to lift the **Organization of American States (OAS)** embargo to encourage internal reform in the Dominican Republic.
- The **OAS’s role as a diplomatic tool** was reinforced; its mechanisms were effectively used for multilateral pressure.

#### **3. Alliance for Progress Credibility**
- The Dominican episode tested the viability of JFK’s **Alliance for Progress** as more than rhetoric.
- Demonstrated the **alignment of diplomacy, soft power, and strategic deterrence** in U.S. foreign policy.

#### **4. Message to Other Dictatorships**
- U.S. support for a democratic transition in the Dominican Republic **signaled to other Latin American regimes** that U.S. backing wasn’t unconditional.
- Emphasized **pluralistic governance** over strategic convenience (as had been common during previous administrations).

#### **5. Long-Term Democratic Experiment**
- Despite ups and downs post-Ramfis, the Trujillo exit laid the groundwork for eventual **electoral governance** in the Dominican Republic (e.g., 1966 election of Joaquín Balaguer).
- This episode served as a **test case** for U.S. crisis management in post-authoritarian transitions in Latin America.

---

### **IV. Conclusion**
The Dominican Republic crisis represented a complex convergence of Cold War geopolitics, post-colonial authoritarian collapse, and U.S. strategic balancing. Kennedy’s administration handled the transition with a mix of **force readiness, strategic diplomacy, and soft power**, reinforcing U.S. influence in the region without direct intervention.

This outcome **enhanced the U.S.'s regional standing**, helped stabilize a potentially volatile state, and tested the Alliance for Progress’s legitimacy in action. The crisis marked a **turning point** in how Washington would engage with Latin American nations facing political upheaval—**favoring controlled transitions and multilateralism over unilateral force**.
